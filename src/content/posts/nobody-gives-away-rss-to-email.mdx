This site has shipped an RSS feed since the first deploy, `feed.xml` and `feed.json`, regenerated on
every build. That serves the people who already run a feed reader, a smaller crowd every year.
Everyone else has to remember I exist. I wanted the unglamorous fix: a form at the end of a post, an
email when a new one goes up, no work from me per post, and a bill as close to $0 as the market
allows.

Those two constraints look unremarkable sitting next to each other. Together they rule out every
reputable provider on the market.

## The provider that looked free

Buttondown was the pick, and the reasoning held up until it didn't. The free tier covered 100
subscribers, and its RSS-to-email pointed at a feed I already ship. The signup was a plain HTML form
with no third-party script on the page, and a CSV export meant the list would never be held hostage.
I built the form to match the site, wired it into the post layout, and verified it in a browser.

The account was live and the integration was finished before Buttondown mentioned the catch:
_RSS-to-email isn't included in your current plan (Free)_.

[Buttondown's own pricing page](https://buttondown.com/pricing) is unambiguous about it. Free gets
you 100 subscribers, a custom sending domain, and hosted archives. RSS-to-email is a +$9/month
add-on, and general automations are +$29. The single feature that made the whole thing
publish-and-forget sits behind that add-on, and it was the only reason to pick them over anyone
else.

A search says otherwise. Review and comparison sites have it as included on free, and they rank
above the pricing page itself.

## The rest of the market says the same thing

[Kit](https://kit.com/pricing) is the best pure-cost answer and fails the other constraint. Free
carries 10,000 subscribers with unlimited broadcasts, which is a genuinely long runway, but RSS
campaigns start on the $33/month Creator plan. Until then you hand-compose a broadcast for every
post, which is precisely the per-post effort I was trying to delete.

[Cloudflare Email Service](https://developers.cloudflare.com/email-service/platform/pricing/) looked
natural, since the site already deploys as a Worker. Sending to arbitrary recipients requires the
$5/month Workers Paid plan. That is cheap, and it is not zero.

[Resend](https://resend.com/pricing) caps its free tier at 100 emails a day and 3,000 a month. As a
newsletter platform, that pins the list at roughly 100 people per send, which is why I dismissed it
early. Behind a list I own, where its whole job is one batch per published post, the same numbers
are plenty.

Free RSS-to-email under your own branding does not exist. It is a paid feature at every provider
worth using, so $0 and zero per-post effort cannot both come off a shelf. They can both come out of
a Worker.

## The UX fix that turned into the migration

The first prototype posted straight to Buttondown's embed endpoint with `no-cors`, which hands back
an opaque response by design. The form could not distinguish a successful signup from a rejected
address, so it showed "check your inbox" either way. Double opt-in makes that technically honest,
since the confirmation email is the real gate, and it still bothered me enough to redo it.

The replacement routed submissions through a first-party `/newsletter/subscribe` route on the Worker
that already fronts the site. It is same-origin, so the CORS problem disappears, and the API key
stays server-side where the browser never sees it. The form gets the true state back and renders it
inline: green for a new signup, violet for an address already on the list, red for a bad one.

That was a user-experience change. It also turned out to be the migration. When Buttondown fell out,
the frontend did not change by a single line. The route's backend went from calling their API to
writing a row in D1, and the form never noticed.

## What it does now

- **Subscribe.** `POST /newsletter/subscribe` writes a `pending` row in D1 and sends a double-opt-in
  confirmation through Resend. Zero JS required: a native form post gets a 303 back to the
  originating post carrying `?subscribe=<state>`, which the form's own script renders and then
  scrubs from the URL.
- **Confirm and unsubscribe.** Both are unguessable token links. Unsubscribe also answers the
  [RFC 8058](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc8058.html) one-click POST that mail clients fire from
  their native button.
- **Notify.** An hourly Cron Trigger diffs `feed.json` against a `sent_posts` table and batch-emails
  confirmed subscribers on anything new.
- **Seed on the first run.** That first tick records the entire current catalog as delivered and
  sends nothing, because the alternative is mailing the whole archive to everybody the moment it
  deploys.

Total cost is $0 inside the free tiers for D1, Workers, Cron Triggers, and Resend.

## The documentation was the deliverable, and it was wrong

Code was the easy part. `docs/newsletter-setup.md` is what somebody has to follow to stand this up,
and it shipped with five errors, each one surfacing the moment the commands got run for real:

- `wrangler d1 execute` with no location flag runs against the **local** database. I documented the
  unflagged form as the remote apply, so the command cheerfully reported
  `5 commands executed successfully` while production stayed empty.
- `wrangler d1 create` is interactive now. My instructions skipped its prompts entirely, and
  answering yes to "would you like Wrangler to add it on your behalf?" writes a second
  `d1_databases` block with a binding name the code does not read.
- The Resend key needs **Sending access** restricted to the sending domain, not Full access. The
  domain restriction is only offered once you pick Sending access, so getting this wrong hands a
  leaked key the run of your account.
- `npx tsx --test` fails in this repo. `pnpm exec tsx --test` is the form that works, and the broken
  one was in the doc and in the test file's own header comment.
- `wrangler dev` is a long-running server, not a command that returns. The doc stacked it in one
  block with the commands that need a second terminal, so it read as a hang.

Every one of those came from writing tool instructions from memory instead of running them first. A
wrong command in a setup doc costs the reader more than a wrong line of code does, because the code
fails loudly and the command succeeds at the wrong thing.

## The wrangler bug that ate an hour

Applying the schema to the remote database failed against a token holding `d1 (write)` and Super
Administrator on the account:

```text
✘ [ERROR] A request to the Cloudflare API (/accounts/.../d1/database/.../import) failed.

  Authentication error [code: 10000]
```

`--file` routes through D1's import endpoint, which rejects wrangler's OAuth login token no matter
how much that token is otherwise allowed to do. `--command` goes through the normal query endpoint,
the same one `wrangler deploy` already authenticates against, so inlining the schema sidesteps the
bug:

```bash
npx wrangler d1 execute meese-rs-newsletter --remote \
  --command "$(grep -v '^[[:space:]]*--' worker/schema.sql)"
```

The `grep` is load-bearing. A schema file opens with `--` SQL comments, and wrangler parses a
leading `--` as a CLI flag, so the naive `$(cat …)` version dies on its own header. If you would
rather keep `--file`, generate a Cloudflare API token from the dashboard and set
`CLOUDFLARE_API_TOKEN`, because token auth does not hit the import bug at all.

## A review found twelve things

Before merging [PR #11](https://github.com/ajmeese7/meese.rs/pull/11) I ran an adversarial review
over the branch, and it came back with twelve issues. Four of them were the kind that lands on
somebody else instead of on me.

**Subscribe was an unauthenticated button that makes me send mail.** It had no throttle whatsoever,
so anyone could burn the Resend daily cap in seconds and take the sending domain's reputation down
with it. Worse, a repeat submit for an address already pending performed no database write and
simply re-sent, so one stranger's inbox could be hammered indefinitely. It now sits behind a
Cloudflare rate limiting binding at 5 per minute per IP, checked before D1 or Resend are touched,
plus a five-minute per-address cooldown on confirmation resends. That cooldown is the layer a per-IP
limiter cannot give you against a rotating source.

**Confirm and unsubscribe mutated state on GET.** Mail security scanners (Outlook Safe Links,
Proofpoint, Barracuda) prefetch every link in an inbound message, so readers were being silently
unsubscribed and confirmations were being recorded against consent nobody had given. Both routes
render a one-button form on GET now, and the row changes on POST. The RFC 8058 one-click header
already POSTs, so mail clients are unaffected.

**Unsubscribing was reversible by an old email.** `confirmByToken` flipped any matching row to
`confirmed` regardless of its current status, and confirm tokens never expire. Clicking a months-old
confirmation link therefore re-subscribed someone who had since opted out. It refuses an
unsubscribed row now, and an already-confirmed one is idempotent, keeping the original
`confirmed_at` instead of recording a re-click as fresh consent.

**The cron dropped recipients and reported success.** It sent one Resend request per subscriber,
sequentially, then recorded the post as sent whether or not those requests worked. Resend's default
limit is 2 requests per second, so past a handful of subscribers the loop reliably produces 429s.
Because the post was recorded regardless, every subscriber whose send failed never got it, with a
log line as the only trace. Recipients go out through the batch endpoint now, up to 100 per request,
and per-recipient headers mean each one still carries their own unsubscribe token. A `deliveries`
table records one row per post-and-subscriber pair, so a retry only targets the people who are
missing. A post that still owes mail is retried on the next hourly tick, up to three attempts, then
abandoned with a logged error rather than retried forever.

## No mocks, which decided the test harness

Only the pure helpers had coverage. Everything holding state was untested: the consent transitions,
the retry and dedup logic, the honeypot, and the first-run seeding guard, which is the single path
where a bug mails the entire back catalog to every subscriber.

The cheap route was a `node:sqlite` adapter behind the D1 interface, letting the existing
`tsx --test` suite keep running in plain Node. `seedSentPosts` and `recordDeliveries` both use D1's
`batch()`, and hand-rolling batch semantics means inventing them. That is a mock wearing a costume,
and a test that agrees with a wrong assumption about a binding is worse than no test at all.

The suite moved to
[`@cloudflare/vitest-pool-workers`](https://developers.cloudflare.com/workers/testing/vitest-integration/)
instead, so tests run inside workerd against the real D1 binding and the real rate limiter.
`test/resend-stub.ts` is a real HTTP server standing in for Resend and for `feed.json`, started once
per run and reached over the network exactly as the live API is. The send path under test is
therefore the production one. The suite is 73 tests and finishes in seconds.

<Callout type="warning" title="The pool loads .dev.vars">
  Left alone, a `vitest-pool-workers` suite inherits whatever real API keys are sitting in
  `.dev.vars` and will happily send live email to any address a test invents. `testEnv()` builds an
  explicit environment pointed at the stub, so no test can reach the real Resend.
</Callout>

Splitting the review fixes into four commits read cleaner in the log. Checking out each one in a
worktree and running the suite found the problem with that: one of the four was red. The tests
substantiating the retry-safety claim had landed a commit later than the change they proved, and the
old suite covered a function that change deleted. Two commits became one.

## The emails looked like a receipt

Resend's default template is light-mode generic, which on a site built around a graphite palette
reads as another company's mail. Both templates use the site shell now: the graphite card on
near-black, the mono `// NEW POST` label that anchors every surface here, a Space Grotesk heading,
IBM Plex body copy, and the violet button lifted from the signup form.

One decision came out of the form's own promise. Pulling Space Grotesk from a font CDN would ping
that host the moment an email opens, which is functionally the tracking pixel the signup copy says
there isn't. The brand fonts sit first in the stack and render for anyone who has them installed
locally; everyone else gets system fallbacks and no network request.

Mailing myself the same test post over and over produced one scare worth writing down, and it turned
out to be Gmail's doing rather than the template's.

<Callout type="tip" title="Gmail trims repeated test sends">
  Gmail threads messages by subject and hides content identical to something already in that
  conversation, so mailing yourself the same post email five times gets four of them trimmed behind
  "Show trimmed content". Real subscribers get one unique subject per post, and the `sent_posts`
  table blocks a repeat, so nobody outside your own testing will ever see it. Tweak the subject line
  and it renders in full.
</Callout>

## The $650 gray circle

The sender avatar in Gmail is a gray letter tile, and the standard for putting a logo there is BIMI
(Brand Indicators for Message Identification, a DNS record pointing at a hosted logo). Gmail will
not honor it without a certificate. A Verified Mark Certificate runs roughly $1,350 to $1,750 a year
and demands a registered trademark; a Common Mark Certificate runs roughly $650 to $1,100 without
one. Paying that so a personal newsletter shows a logomark instead of a circle is not a close call.

The free path is real and it shipped, in [its own PR](https://github.com/ajmeese7/meese.rs/pull/12)
because it is DNS and a static asset rather than application code. It needs DMARC at enforcement, an
SVG Tiny PS version of the logomark, and one TXT record at `default._bimi`. That buys Yahoo, AOL,
and Fastmail. Gmail and Apple Mail both keep showing the gray circle.

Apple deserves a flag here, because their documentation contradicts itself. The
[consumer support page](https://support.apple.com/en-us/108340) says a VMC is "not strictly
required", which reads like self-asserted BIMI works. The
[sender-facing developer page](https://developer.apple.com/support/bimi) lists a verified BIMI
Evidence Document as a required step. Take the developer page.

Proton does not implement BIMI at all. It fetches the sender's favicon server-side and displays
that, so `public/favicon.svg` is what governs there and the record is irrelevant.

I also undersold the scope when I first pitched the free path as an afternoon. The SVG and the DNS
record genuinely are one. Moving DMARC from `p=none` to enforcement safely means running aggregate
reporting for a couple of weeks first, to confirm everything sending as the domain passes alignment
before anything starts getting quarantined. Calendar-wise that is two weeks, not an afternoon.

The bill is $0, against the $9/month add-on that started all of this. The form at the bottom of this
post is the thing itself.
