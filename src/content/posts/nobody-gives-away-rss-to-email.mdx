This site has shipped an [RSS feed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RSS) since the first deploy,
`feed.xml` and `feed.json`, regenerated on every build. That serves the people who already run a
feed reader, a smaller crowd every year. Everyone else has to remember I exist. I wanted the
unglamorous fix: a form at the end of a post, and an email to whoever fills it in when a new one
goes up. Two things constrained it. It had to cost as close to $0 as the market allows, and it had
to take no work from me per post.

Neither constraint is unusual on its own. Together they rule out every reputable provider on the
market.

## The provider that looked free

Buttondown was the pick, and the reasoning held up until it didn't. The free tier covered 100
subscribers, and its RSS-to-email pointed at a feed I already ship. The signup was a plain HTML form
with no third-party script on the page, and a CSV export meant the list would never be held hostage.
I built the form to match the site, wired it into the post layout, and verified it in a browser.

The account was live and the integration was finished before Buttondown mentioned the catch:
_RSS-to-email isn't included in your current plan (Free)_.

[Buttondown's own pricing page](https://buttondown.com/pricing) is unambiguous about it. Free gets
you 100 subscribers, a custom sending domain, and hosted archives. RSS-to-email is a +$9/month
add-on, and general automations are +$29. The single feature that made the whole thing
publish-and-forget sits behind that add-on, and it was the only reason to pick them over anyone
else.

A search says otherwise. Review and comparison sites have it as included on free, and they rank
above the pricing page itself. That is also the corpus an AI agent draws on, so asking one returns
the same wrong answer with more confidence attached, which is exactly what came back when I had one
compare providers for me. Pricing and feature availability need reading off the vendor's own page,
every time, whatever handed you the summary.

## The rest of the market

[Kit](https://kit.com/pricing) is the best answer on cost and the worst on effort. Free carries
10,000 subscribers with unlimited broadcasts, which is a genuinely long runway, but RSS campaigns
start on the $33/month Creator plan. Until then you hand-compose a broadcast for every post, which
is precisely the per-post effort I was trying to delete.

[Cloudflare Email Service](https://developers.cloudflare.com/email-service/platform/pricing/) looked
natural, since the site already deploys as a Worker. Sending to arbitrary recipients requires the
$5/month Workers Paid plan. That is cheap, and it is not zero.

[Resend](https://resend.com/pricing) is not a newsletter platform at all, which turns out to be the
useful part. It is an API that sends what you hand it, so there is no RSS automation for it to put
behind a plan, because the schedule and the subscriber list would be mine to build. Its free tier
caps at 100 emails a day and 3,000 a month, and that ceiling is real no matter who owns the list.

All four split the same way. Where RSS-to-email exists it is a paid feature, and where it is free it
does not exist, because those products are send infrastructure with no scheduler attached. $0 and
zero per-post effort cannot both come off a shelf. They can both come out of a Worker.

## The UX fix that turned into the migration

The first prototype posted straight to Buttondown's embed endpoint with `no-cors`, which hands back
an opaque response by design. The form could not distinguish a successful signup from a rejected
address, so it showed "check your inbox" either way. Double opt-in makes that technically honest,
since the confirmation email is the real gate, and it still bothered me enough to redo it.

The replacement routed submissions through a first-party `/newsletter/subscribe` route on the Worker
that already fronts the site. It is same-origin, so the CORS problem disappears, and the API key
stays server-side where the browser never sees it. The form gets the true state back and renders it
inline: green for a new signup, violet for an address already on the list, red for a bad one.

That was a user-experience change. It also turned out to be the migration. When Buttondown fell out,
the frontend did not change by a single line. The route's backend went from calling their API to
writing a row in D1, and the form never noticed.

## What it does now

- **Subscribe.** `POST /newsletter/subscribe` writes a `pending` row in D1 and sends a double-opt-in
  confirmation through Resend. Signing up survives having scripts blocked, because the form is a
  native POST and the Worker sends a 303 back to the post it came from. The inline status message is
  the part that needs JavaScript.
- **Confirm and unsubscribe.** Both are unguessable token links. Unsubscribe also answers the
  [RFC 8058](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc8058.html) one-click POST that mail clients fire from
  their native button.
- **Notify.** An hourly Cron Trigger diffs `feed.json` against a `sent_posts` table and batch-emails
  confirmed subscribers on anything new.
- **Seed on the first run.** That first tick records the entire current catalog as delivered and
  sends nothing, because the alternative is mailing the whole archive to everybody the moment it
  deploys.

Total cost is $0 inside the free tiers for D1, Workers, Cron Triggers, and Resend. The binding limit
is Resend's 100 emails a day, which caps the list at roughly the size Buttondown hosts for free, so
this does not dodge the ceiling. What it changes is what the ceiling costs: Buttondown wants +$9 a
month before it will send on its own, and this sends on its own from the first post. Past the
ceiling I pay Resend for volume instead of paying Buttondown for automation.

## Setup steps where the obvious command misleads you

Code was the easy part. `docs/newsletter-setup.md` is what somebody has to follow to stand this up,
and four of its steps needed correcting once the commands met a real terminal:

- `wrangler d1 execute` runs against the **local** database unless you pass `--remote`. Leave the
  flag off and it still prints `5 commands executed successfully`, so a schema you meant to apply to
  production reads as applied while the production database sits empty.
- `wrangler d1 create` is interactive. Answering yes to "would you like Wrangler to add it on your
  behalf?" appends its own `d1_databases` block, with a binding name your code does not read, on top
  of the one already in `wrangler.jsonc`.
- The Resend key needs **Sending access** restricted to the one domain you send from, not Full
  access. The domain restriction is only offered once you pick Sending access, so the wrong choice
  hands a leaked key the run of your account.
- `wrangler dev` is a long-running server, not a command that returns, so everything after it in a
  walkthrough belongs in a second terminal. Stacked into one block, it reads as a hang.

A bad command in a setup doc costs more than a bad line of code, because code fails loudly and a
command succeeds at the wrong thing. Every one of these came from writing the doc against a
recollection of how these tools behave instead of against the versions sitting on disk.

## A D1 schema apply that fails on a valid token

Applying the schema to the production database is
`wrangler d1 execute --remote --file worker/schema.sql`. Against a token holding `d1 (write)` and
Super Administrator on the account, it fails:

```text
✘ [ERROR] A request to the Cloudflare API (/accounts/.../d1/database/.../import) failed.

  Authentication error [code: 10000]
```

The failing request is to D1's import endpoint, which is the path `--file` takes. `--command` goes
through the ordinary query endpoint instead, the one `wrangler deploy` already authenticates
against, and that succeeds on the same login. So inlining the schema sidesteps it:

```bash
npx wrangler d1 execute meese-rs-newsletter --remote \
  --command "$(grep -v '^[[:space:]]*--' worker/schema.sql)"
```

The `grep` is load-bearing. A schema file opens with `--` SQL comments, and wrangler parses a
leading `--` as a CLI flag, so handing it the raw file contents with `$(cat worker/schema.sql)` dies
on the header. Generating a Cloudflare API token from the dashboard and setting
`CLOUDFLARE_API_TOKEN` clears the error too, and `--file` then works. Two different credentials
against the same account, one path refusing and the other not, points at the import endpoint
declining the OAuth login token rather than at any missing scope.

## A review found reader choices overwritten and posts never delivered

Before merging [PR #11](https://github.com/ajmeese7/meese.rs/pull/11) I ran an adversarial review
over the whole change, and it came back with twelve issues. Eight were the ordinary kind, stale
comments and duplicated constants and a hand-inlined component. The other four would have been felt
by subscribers rather than by me. A broken build announces itself; these would have run quietly for
months.

**The subscribe form was a public button for making my site send mail.** It had no throttle
whatsoever, so anyone could burn the Resend daily cap in seconds and take the sending domain's
reputation down with it. Worse, a repeat submit for an address already pending performed no database
write and simply re-sent, so one stranger's inbox could be hammered indefinitely. It now sits behind
a Cloudflare rate limiting binding at 5 per minute per IP, checked before D1 or Resend are touched,
plus a five-minute per-address cooldown on confirmation resends. That cooldown is the layer a per-IP
limiter cannot give you against a rotating source.

**Confirm and unsubscribe mutated state on GET.** Mail security scanners (Outlook Safe Links,
Proofpoint, Barracuda) prefetch every link in an inbound message, so a scanner could drop a reader
off the list without them touching anything, or record a confirmation nobody had granted. Both
routes render a one-button form on GET now, and the row changes on POST. The RFC 8058 one-click
header already POSTs, so mail clients are unaffected.

**Unsubscribing was reversible by an old email.** The confirm handler promoted any row its token
matched, whatever state that row was in, and confirm tokens never expire. Someone who unsubscribed
and later clicked the original confirmation link, out of a months-old email, was quietly
re-subscribed. It refuses an unsubscribed row now, and a second click on an already-confirmed one
changes nothing, so the row still shows when they actually signed up rather than when they last
clicked a link.

**The cron dropped recipients and reported success.** It sent one Resend request per subscriber,
sequentially, then recorded the post as sent whether or not those requests worked. A loop like that
pushes against two ceilings as a list grows, since
[Resend rate-limits a team to 10 requests per second](https://resend.com/docs/api-reference/rate-limit)
and a Worker gets a fixed subrequest budget per invocation. The bookkeeping was the worse half:
because the post was recorded regardless of the outcome, every subscriber whose send failed never
got that post, with a log line as the only trace. Recipients go out through the batch endpoint now,
up to 100 per request, and per-recipient headers mean each one still carries their own unsubscribe
token. A `deliveries` table records one row per post-and-subscriber pair, so a retry only targets
the people who are missing. A post that still owes mail goes again on the next hourly tick, up to
three attempts, then is abandoned with a logged error rather than retried forever.

## Why the tests run inside Cloudflare's own runtime

Tests at that point covered only the functions that take an input and return an output, which is the
easy half. Nothing that touched the database had one: whether an unsubscribed reader can be
resurrected, whether a retry sends twice, whether the hidden field that catches bots really discards
what it catches. The worst gap was the first-run seeding guard, the check that stops a fresh deploy
from mailing every archived post to every subscriber at once.

Testing those needs a database. The cheap way to get one is an adapter that wraps `node:sqlite` in
the interface D1 exposes, so the tests keep running in plain Node. Seeding and delivery-recording
both lean on D1's `batch()`. Writing that adapter means deciding for myself what it guarantees:
whether the statements share a transaction, and whether a failure partway through rolls back the
ones before it. Get that wrong and the tests still go green, because they are confirming my
assumption rather than the runtime's behavior. That is worse than no coverage at all, because it
reports success while the bug ships.

The alternative is to stop substituting and run the real thing.
[`@cloudflare/vitest-pool-workers`](https://developers.cloudflare.com/workers/testing/vitest-integration/)
executes the suite inside workerd, the same runtime Cloudflare runs in production, against a real D1
binding and a real rate limiter. Resend is the one thing that cannot be real, since a test suite
must not send mail, so `test/resend-stub.ts` is a small HTTP server that answers on the same
endpoints. The Worker calls it over the network exactly as it would the live API, which leaves the
send path under test identical to the shipped one. That is 73 tests, and they finish in about six
seconds.

<Callout type="warning" title="The pool loads .dev.vars">
  Left alone, a `vitest-pool-workers` suite inherits whatever real API keys are sitting in
  `.dev.vars` and will happily send live email to any address a test invents. The suite builds its
  environment explicitly, pointed at the stub, so no test can reach the real Resend.
</Callout>

Worth checking out each commit in a worktree and running the tests against it before pushing, too. A
tidy-looking split had put the tests that prove the retry behavior one commit later than the retry
change itself, which left a commit in the middle where the suite was red.

## The emails looked like a receipt

Resend's default template is light-mode generic, which on a site built around a graphite palette
reads as another company's mail. Both templates use the site shell now: the graphite card on
near-black, the mono `// NEW POST` label that anchors every surface here, a Space Grotesk heading,
IBM Plex body copy, and the violet button lifted from the signup form.

One decision came out of the form's own promise. Pulling Space Grotesk from a font CDN would ping
that host the moment an email opens, which is functionally the tracking pixel the signup copy says
there isn't. The brand fonts sit first in the stack and render for anyone who has them installed
locally; everyone else gets system fallbacks and no network request.

Mailing myself the same test post over and over produced one scare worth writing down, and it turned
out to be Gmail's doing rather than the template's.

<Callout type="tip" title="Gmail trims repeated test sends">
  Gmail threads messages by subject and hides content identical to something already in that
  conversation, so mailing yourself the same post email five times gets four of them trimmed behind
  "Show trimmed content". Real subscribers get one unique subject per post, and the `sent_posts`
  table blocks a repeat, so nobody outside your own testing will ever see it. Tweak the subject line
  and it renders in full.
</Callout>

## The $650 gray circle

The sender avatar in Gmail is a gray letter tile, and the standard for putting a logo there is BIMI
(Brand Indicators for Message Identification, a DNS record pointing at a hosted logo). Gmail will
not honor it without a certificate, and there are two. A Verified Mark Certificate costs somewhere
around $1,350 to $1,750 a year and demands a registered trademark. Without a trademark, the Common
Mark variety still runs $650 to $1,100. Paying either so a personal newsletter shows a logomark
instead of a circle is not a close call.

The free path is real and it shipped, in [its own PR](https://github.com/ajmeese7/meese.rs/pull/12)
because it is DNS and a static asset rather than application code. It needs DMARC at enforcement, an
SVG Tiny PS version of the logomark, and one TXT record at `default._bimi`. That buys Yahoo, AOL,
and Fastmail. Gmail and Apple Mail both keep showing the gray circle.

Apple deserves a flag here, because their documentation contradicts itself. The
[consumer support page](https://support.apple.com/en-us/108340) says a VMC is "not strictly
required", which reads as though the free path is enough. The
[sender-facing developer page](https://developer.apple.com/support/bimi) lists a verified BIMI
Evidence Document as a required step. Take the developer page.

Proton does not implement BIMI at all. It fetches the sender's favicon server-side and displays
that, so `public/favicon.svg` is what governs there and the record is irrelevant.

DMARC sets the schedule here, not the hands-on work. Enforcement means `p=quarantine` or `p=reject`,
and flipping to either one blind will send legitimate mail from the domain to spam. The safe order
is to turn on aggregate reporting, wait until it shows everything sending as the domain passing
alignment, and only then raise the policy. That waiting is a couple of weeks.

The bill is $0, against the $9/month add-on that started all of this. The form at the bottom of this
post is the thing itself.
