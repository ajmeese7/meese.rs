The question I started with was about orientation. An ant on the floor has gravity pressing it into
the surface. An ant on a wall has gravity trying to drag it down the surface. An ant upside down on
a ceiling has gravity trying to pull it straight off. So a wall should be harder to grip than a
floor, and a ceiling harder still, but I wanted a number for how _much_ harder.

The number turned out to be boring, the reason for which is the actual interesting part. When an ant
is fully gripped onto a smooth surface, it can resist tens to hundreds of times its own body weight.
Gravity only ever costs it one body weight. Subtracting one from several hundred does almost
nothing, so the floor-to-wall penalty mostly disappears. What actually decides whether the wind wins
is not the angle of the surface. It is the _failure mode_ the wind is allowed to exploit, and
whether the ant is gripped at all.

This is a modeling piece, not an experiment. Due to budgetary constraints, nobody put these ants in
a wind tunnel. The grip measurements come from published biomechanics work, and I am running them
through the standard drag equation to convert "force the ant can resist" into "wind speed that would
produce that force." Every assumption is on the table below, and the interactive pieces use the same
math throughout.

## What gets measured, and what does not

Ant feet are not one thing. Each tarsus (the foot segment) carries a soft adhesive pad called the
arolium plus arrays of stiff tarsal hairs, and the two do different jobs.
[Endlein and Federle (2015)](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0141269) pulled this apart in the
weaver ant _Oecophylla smaragdina_: the hair arrays generated friction of about **3.1 times body
weight** on smooth surfaces, but their pure adhesion (resisting a straight pull away from the
surface) was **less than half a body weight**. Hairs are good at shear and bad at hanging. The
arolium is what does the real adhesive holding.

The best cross-species dataset is
[Stark, Arstingstall, and Yanoviak (2018)](https://doi.org/10.1242/jeb.171843). They pulled 580
workers from 11 tropical arboreal species along a vertical glass plate and recorded the maximum
shear load before the ant slid. The glass was deliberately smoother than the ants' claw tips, which
takes claws out of the picture and isolates pad-and-secretion grip. Those are the numbers worth
modeling, with one caveat I will keep repeating: they are best-case, fully-engaged,
clean-smooth-glass numbers.

Relative grip rewards the small. A featherweight _Crematogaster_ holds hundreds of times its own
weight; the giant bullet ant _Paraponera clavata_, two thousand times heavier, manages about 21.
Flip to absolute force and the ranking inverts, because 21 times a big number still beats 600 times
a tiny one. Neither view is wrong. They answer different questions, and the wind question cares
about the third tab.

## Turning grip into wind

Aerodynamic drag follows the
[standard quadratic form](https://www1.grc.nasa.gov/beginners-guide-to-aeronautics/drag-equation/):

```
F = ½ · ρ · C_d · A · v²
```

Solve for the wind speed that matches a given resisting force:

```
v = √( 2·F / (ρ · C_d · A) )
```

`F` is the grip the ant can hold, in newtons. `ρ` is air density, `C_d` is the drag coefficient, `A`
is the ant's frontal area facing the wind. The output `v` is the steady wind that would generate
exactly that much drag on the body.

<Callout type="context" title="The assumptions, stated once">
  Air density `ρ = 1.225 kg/m³` (sea level). Drag coefficient `C_d = 1.0`, the round figure for a
  bluff, non-streamlined body. Frontal area scales isometrically from a reference ant (3.0 mm² for
  an 8.2 mg worker, `A = A_ref · (m/m_ref)^⅔`). These are deliberately plain choices. The point is
  to compare apples to apples across species, not to claim a wind-tunnel-certified threshold for any
  one ant.
</Callout>

Run _Crematogaster crinosa_ through it (1.03 mg, 0.7 g max shear load) and you get 122 m/s, about
**273 mph**. That is past the
[strongest surface wind gust ever reliably recorded on Earth](https://wmo.asu.edu/content/world-maximum-surface-wind-gust)
(253 mph, Cyclone Olivia, 1996). A fully gripped ant on clean glass is, in this model, harder to
blow off than any wind we have ever measured. That is the headline, and it is also where the honesty
has to kick in, because "fully gripped on clean glass" is doing enormous work in that sentence.

## Why the wind speeds stay so high

The square in the drag equation cuts both ways. Going from `F` to `v` means taking a square root,
which compresses the spread hard. A tenfold increase in grip buys you only about 3.2 times the wind
speed. Drag it around:

<DampingCurve />

This is why the species table reads 108 to 273 mph instead of spanning two orders of magnitude the
way the raw grip ratios (21 to 680) do. A 30-fold range in grip becomes less than a 3-fold range in
wind. It is also why arguing about whether an ant holds 200 or 400 body weights barely moves the
wind number. The square root does not care.

## Where orientation actually lives

Now the part I went in for. On a floor, gravity presses the ant down into the surface and the wind
has to beat the full shear grip to slide it sideways. On a wall, gravity pulls along the surface, so
it spends one body weight out of the shear budget before the wind shows up. The wall threshold
relative to the floor is `√((R−1)/R)`, where `R` is grip in body weights. For the bullet ant at
`R ≈ 21`, that is a 2.4% reduction. For _Crematogaster_ at `R ≈ 680`, it is 0.07%. The wall is not
meaningfully harder than the floor for a gripped ant.

The ceiling is where it changes, and not because of one extra body weight. It changes because the
failure mode changes. Slide the surface around and watch which forces are fighting which:

<OrientationWidget />

On the floor and the wall, the wind is trying to make the ant _slide_, and shear grip resists
sliding. On the ceiling, gravity pulls the ant straight off the surface, so survival depends on
normal adhesion and peel resistance instead of shear. This is exactly where the hair arrays fail:
strong in shear, weak in pull-off. An ant relying on hair friction can hold a wall and fall off a
ceiling, and the calculator shows it. Put the calculator on "ceiling," set the reserve to "tarsal
hairs only," and the readout drops to zero, because half a body weight of adhesion cannot even hold
the ant's own weight upside down, let alone fight wind. The arolium is what makes ceilings
survivable, and ceiling performance swings from "falls under gravity alone" to "shrugs off a
hurricane" depending entirely on how much pad is in contact.

So orientation matters, but it matters by switching which capacity is on the hook, not by stacking
up gravity penalties.

## What this is not

The high numbers are upper bounds, and treating them as field thresholds would be wrong. A few
reasons the real world is gentler:

- **Clean glass is a lie.** Stark et al. used it on purpose to isolate pad grip. Real bark and
  leaves are wet, dusty, waxy, and rough.
  [Roughness can help by giving claws something to catch, or hurt by keeping the pad from making contact](https://doi.org/10.1093/icb/icaa078).
  The sign depends on the scale of the texture versus the scale of the foot.
- **Walking ants are not gripped ants.** The big numbers assume all six tarsi engaged and the body
  aligned with the load. An ant mid-stride on three legs, using hair friction rather than seated
  pads, is operating at a small fraction of that and comes off far easier.
- **Wind is not steady.** The model uses constant freestream drag. Real gusts are turbulent, they
  apply torque, and a peeling moment can start a detachment at a force well below the straight-pull
  threshold. The ceiling estimates are the most optimistic for exactly this reason.
- **Near a surface, the air slows down.** An ant pressed flush to a wall sits inside the boundary
  layer where local air speed is well below freestream, which pushes the _real_ wind needed even
  higher than the isolated-body model says.
- **The drag coefficient is a stand-in.** `C_d = 1.0` is the round number for a bluff body, but a
  real ant is hairy and trails legs and antennae into the flow, so its true coefficient is probably
  higher and changes with which way it faces the wind. That pushes the real threshold down, but only
  as the square root of `C_d`, so even doubling or tripling it leaves the headline in extreme-wind
  territory.

The defensible version of the answer is conditional. A casually walking ant on a wet or dusty
surface can be moved by a strong gust. A fully braced ant on a favorable surface resists forces
that, converted to wind, land in hurricane territory and beyond. The orientation it is sitting at
barely enters into it, until the surface goes overhead and the whole problem becomes about peel
instead of slide.
